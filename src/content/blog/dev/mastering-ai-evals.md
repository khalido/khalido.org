## Traditional Metrics Don't Work for AI

There is a mix of outputs - you can have AI outputs which are non-deterministic but lead to numbers or probabilities to which we can apply deterministic metrics - but often companies are using generative outputs where traditional metrics fail.

Think Accuracy, Tone & Style, Format, Safety & Completeness - all things which aren't easy to measure.

For generated text, using LLMs as a judge is often the answer. One interesting point - how do you eval the judge? One way is to log all the interactions and judgements and have another flow to highlight potential improvements for the eval judge - things it missed or over indexed on or policy gaps etc.

Judges need to be evaluated and can be unreliable!

:::ai
2025–2026 research has quantified several consistent failure modes in LLM judges:

- **Positional bias**: Judges flip their verdict in ~45% of decisive pairwise comparisons when answer order is swapped — worst precisely when response quality is close, which is when you need the judge most.
- **Near-zero inter-judge agreement**: A Jan 2026 study (arXiv 2601.05114) found Krippendorff's α = 0.042 across 9 judges. Judges agree with themselves across runs but not with each other — they're measuring different things, not providing noisy estimates of a shared truth.
- **Self-preference and family bias**: GPT-4o and Claude 3.5 Sonnet both score their own outputs higher after controlling for actual quality, with the bias extending to same-provider models.
- **Verbosity bias**: Longer responses score higher independent of quality, creating an incentive to game LLM-judged leaderboards with verbose but weaker answers.

What actually helps: task-specific evaluation criteria is the highest-value single intervention; chain-of-thought prompting helped all models tested; ensembles of 3–8 judges capture most available gains without fine-tuning. The most important practical rule: **pin your judge model version** — silent upstream updates cause eval scores to drift week over week with no code change on your end.
:::

However, Judge LLMs could be more reliable than the generating LLM: Evaluating can be set up to be a much simpler job than the original generative task for narrow, well-defined tasks with clear rubrics - use golden samples, kick out to a human in the loop for edge cases, check and tweak prompt against human examples.

## Customer facing bot

Think through edge cases and adversarial inputs and eval the output.

You build out a corpus of test cases to check against your bot - a mix of human ones and others generated by a smart LLM. Recommendation - build out the initial ones old school style. Front line staff will have great examples.

Many large companies are using simpler models to check/classify llm generated output before it gets sent out.

Consider models like [gpt-oss-safeguard](https://openai.com/index/introducing-gpt-oss-safeguard/) or services like [Google's Guardrails API](https://developers.google.com/checks/guide/ai-safety/guardrails) to run msgs through a policy as a safeguard:

> gpt-oss-safeguard models use reasoning to directly interpret a developer-provided policy at inference time—classifying user messages, completions, and full chats according to the developer’s needs.

This can be slow - I haven't personally used this yet. For internal facing bots users are known, logged and trained - so there's an expectation of using the bot properly.

## In Closing

Use evals at every step. Evals need to be part of your CI and potentially inside the ongoing work being generated. For now, I don't use any frameworks for evals as my current agentic workflows are very bespoke and I use metrics where possible, simple LLM judges where not to highlight and push to a human anything "tricky". 

:::ai
Hamel Husain ran three tools through the same homework assignment in his [eval tools comparison](https://hamel.dev/blog/posts/eval-tools/) — LangSmith, Braintrust, and Arize Phoenix — and found the tool matters less than the process. His strongest recommendation: use any platform primarily as a data backend, do the actual analysis in notebooks with a custom annotation UI.

The four frameworks practitioners reach for most:

| Framework | What it's for |
|---|---|
| [**Braintrust**](https://www.braintrustdata.com/) | Managed platform — human annotation queues, CI/CD deployment gates that block on regressions, shared team visibility. Best for cross-functional teams. Hamel's caveat: avoid the auto-scorer ("Loop") which stacks abstractions and inflates scores. |
| [**Arize Phoenix**](https://phoenix.arize.com/) | Open-source, locally runnable, OpenTelemetry-native tracing. Data exports to Pandas DataFrames so you can do real analysis in notebooks. The panel's favourite for developer-first work without a SaaS bill. |
| [**DeepEval**](https://docs.confident-ai.com/) | Open-source, pytest-native — runs as a CI/CD gate, returns non-zero exit codes on failure. 50+ built-in metrics covering RAG, agents, safety, and multi-turn conversations. |
| [**RAGAS**](https://docs.ragas.io/) | Purpose-built for RAG pipelines. Measures faithfulness, answer relevancy, context precision and recall — no ground truth required. A library, not a platform; pair it with Phoenix or Langfuse for observability. |

Big-player moves worth noting: OpenAI [acquired Promptfoo](https://www.promptfoo.dev/about) in March 2026 (was the leading open-source red-teaming tool, MIT licence still active but future uncertain); Anthropic acquired Humanloop in August 2025 and absorbed it into the Console's Workbench and Evaluations features.
:::
